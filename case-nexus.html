Nexus Financial Platform

Redesigning the core trading interface and building a scalable design system for a B2B fintech serving over 40,000 institutional users across 12 countries.

01 — The Problem

A fragmented system slowing teams down

Nexus had grown through acquisition, inheriting three separate frontend codebases, each with its own visual language. Engineers were spending 30% of their sprint capacity resolving design inconsistencies instead of shipping features.

Users — institutional traders — reported high cognitive load when switching between modules. The platform failed accessibility audits, and dark mode didn't exist despite near-universal demand.

My mandate: build a unified design system from the ground up, while simultaneously redesigning the primary trading dashboard to meet the needs of power users managing multi-asset portfolios.

The constraint: we could not break existing workflows or require users to relearn the product. Every change had to be backward-compatible in behaviour, while being visually and structurally new.

02 — Discovery

Research that changed the brief

I ran 14 contextual interviews with traders across London, New York, and Singapore over three weeks. The sessions were observational — I watched users work in their actual environment, noting where they hesitated, where they used workarounds, and what they said out loud.

The biggest finding was unexpected: the primary usability issue wasn't the interface — it was the information architecture. Users had developed an elaborate mental model of the platform that didn't match the navigation structure at all. Menus were labelled from an engineering perspective, not a trading workflow perspective.

I synthesised these insights into three user archetypes and a revised task hierarchy that became the north star for the entire redesign.

03 — Design System

Building Nexus DS — 240 components, 2 themes

I architected the design system in Figma using a three-tier token structure: primitive → semantic → component. This meant that theming (light/dark) required changing a single token layer, and the entire system updated automatically.

Every component was documented in Zeroheight with usage guidelines, accessibility requirements (WCAG AA), and code snippets reviewed by the lead engineer.

The data visualisation library was the most complex undertaking — 26 chart variants with consistent interaction patterns, tooltip systems, and colour-blind safe palettes. I established rules for when to use each chart type, embedded directly in the documentation.

The system shipped in three phases over six months, with a dedicated migration guide for the engineering team to transition legacy components.

04 — Outcomes

Measurable impact across every metric
