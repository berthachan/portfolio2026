Using data to identify the problem areas

Initial analytics showed a significant drop-off at the transfer confirmation screen, but the numbers alone didn't explain why. I used a combination of FullStory session recordings, funnel analysis, and exit surveys to triangulate the cause.

Three patterns emerged immediately:

  • Users were pausing and scrolling back repeatedly before confirming — a sign of unresolved uncertainty, not confusion about the UI itself.
  • Fee breakdowns were buried below the fold on the confirmation screen, meaning most users never saw the final cost before committing.
  • The exchange rate display changed between the entry screen and confirmation, creating a perceived inconsistency that eroded trust.
User Feedback — Exit Survey, May 2023

"It is not transparent as to what the fees are or who charged them. It's a guessing game and I sometimes don't know where to find them."

— Card feedback from a user study, 2023

But, research tells us the full story

Data surfaces what is happening. Research explains why. I ran 12 moderated user interviews with existing customers across the UK, Germany, and the UAE — focusing on their mental model of international transfers and what "trust" meant to them in a financial context.

The interviews revealed that users approached transfers with a pre-existing anxiety about hidden costs — shaped by years of bad experiences with legacy banking. Our platform wasn't the cause, but it wasn't actively countering that fear either.

A journey mapping workshop with the product and compliance teams helped surface the business constraints: certain fee disclosures were required at specific flow stages by regulation. The design challenge was making mandatory information feel like a feature, not a legal footnote.

I synthesised all research into a single "friction map" — a visual overlay of the existing flow annotated with user emotion, drop-off data, and regulatory requirements at each step. This became the single source of truth for the redesign brief.

Information Architecture

Defining the skeletal structure. We map the taxonomy and navigation logic before a single pixel of colour is applied.

Prioritise design efforts in high-impact areas

With a clear friction map in hand, I ran a prioritisation workshop with the PM and engineering leads to score each identified problem against impact (drop-off reduction potential) and feasibility (build complexity). Three problem areas rose to the top and formed the scope of the redesign.

Fee Transparency First

Surface the complete fee breakdown — including third-party charges — at the point of entry, not just at confirmation. Users need to commit to a cost, not discover it.

Consistent Rate Display

Exchange rates must be locked and displayed identically across every step of the flow. Rate movement between screens creates perceived deception even when technically accurate.

Reduce Information Overload

Compliance-required copy was overwhelming the key decision signals. Re-architect each screen around a single primary action with secondary information progressively disclosed.

New experience to reduce information overload

The existing confirmation screen had 14 distinct pieces of information competing for attention at the same hierarchy level. I restructured it around a clear information hierarchy: what you're sending → what they receive → what it costs → confirm.

Each piece of compliance copy was reviewed with the legal team to identify the minimum required wording. Where possible, dense paragraphs were replaced with scannable structured data and progressive disclosure patterns.

Problem area by problem area

Rather than a full-screen redesign delivered in one release, I proposed a modular approach: redesign each problem area independently, ship it, validate it, then move to the next. This reduced risk, gave the team early wins to build confidence, and let us measure the impact of each change in isolation.

Each problem area had its own discovery sprint, design sprint, and validation round before handoff. The three areas shipped over 10 weeks.

Interaction & Visuals

Building the high-fidelity interface using a modular design system. Focus on accessibility, rhythm, and reducing cognitive load at every decision point.

Conclusion

The redesign addressed each friction point systematically. The fee transparency change alone — moving the full fee breakdown to the amount entry screen — accounted for the largest single drop in mid-flow abandonment.

The consistent rate display fix was technically simpler but psychologically significant: users in follow-up testing explicitly commented that the platform felt "more honest" even though the underlying rates were identical to before.

Key decisions made during the interaction design phase:

  • A persistent summary panel surfaced throughout the flow, keeping key numbers (amount, rate, fees, total) always visible without needing to scroll back.
  • Compliance text was moved into an inline expandable component — visible on request, not buried in a collapsed accordion at the bottom of the page.
  • A "rate lock" indicator with a countdown timer was introduced to address the anxiety around rate movement between screens.
  • The confirmation screen was rebuilt from 14 parallel information items to a 4-stage visual summary with clear hierarchy.

The Atomic Approach

Design tokens are defined at the root level to ensure seamless implementation for engineers. Every button state, spacing increment, and colour hex is documented. The fee breakdown module and rate lock indicator were added to the shared component library so they can be reused across all transfer types.

Research standardisation and Design hand-off upgrade

A secondary outcome of this project was the establishment of a repeatable research and handoff process. Previously, each design project had its own ad-hoc documentation approach. I used this project to pilot a standardised handoff package that included:

  • An annotated Figma file with edge cases documented inline for each component.
  • A research summary template capturing methodology, key findings, and design implications in a format engineers and PMs could scan in under 5 minutes.
  • A QA checklist linked directly to the acceptance criteria in Jira, covering accessibility, responsiveness, and functional parity with the design spec.

The process was adopted by two other product teams within the quarter following this project's handoff.

The Feedback Loop

Testing isn't a final hurdle; it's a recurring check-in. We prototype, test, and break things early to avoid expensive revisions during development. Each of the three problem areas shipped with its own A/B test to isolate impact before full rollout.

The modular release approach proved its value in the validation phase: because each change was shipped independently, we could attribute +9% of the overall improvement to fee transparency, +12% to the rate consistency fix, and +7% to the confirmation screen restructure.

These numbers became the foundation for the team's next quarterly roadmap — each future initiative now goes through the same friction-mapping and modular prioritisation process established in this project.
