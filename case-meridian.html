01 — The Brief

Building the anti-algorithm travel tool

Meridian launched in 2019 as a content-rich travel guide. By 2021, they had amassed 12,000 editorially verified place recommendations across 60 cities — but no way for users to do anything with that content beyond reading it.

The founders wanted to build a trip-planning layer on top of the existing content: a tool that let users build itineraries from Meridian's curated database, informed by their own preferences rather than popularity rankings.

This was fundamentally a search and discovery problem with high emotional stakes. Travel planning is personal — a failed recommendation can ruin a holiday. The design had to build trust quickly and make it easy for users to express nuanced preferences.

I was brought in as the sole designer to take the product from zero to a functional MVP in five months, working alongside two engineers and the co-founders.

02 — Research & Strategy

Understanding how people actually plan trips

Before touching Figma, I ran a two-week discovery phase: 11 interviews with frequent independent travellers, a diary study with 6 participants planning real upcoming trips, and a competitive audit of 14 platforms including Google Trips (defunct), TripAdvisor, and Notion-based planning templates.

The diary study was the most valuable input. Watching how people actually plan — the tabs they keep open, the notes they take, the Instagram saves they reference — made it clear that existing tools were solving the wrong problem. Nobody needed a better list of restaurants. They needed a way to hold their research together and make sense of it.

This reframe shifted the product strategy from "curated recommendations engine" to "personal trip canvas" — a surface where users could collect, organise, and sequence Meridian's content alongside their own notes and saves.

03 — Key Design Work

The canvas model and preference system

The Trip Canvas. The central metaphor was a spatial canvas — a day-by-day grid where users could drag and drop saved places, add personal notes, and see their itinerary take shape visually. Unlike a list-based planner, the canvas gave a sense of spatial rhythm: mornings, afternoons, evenings, travel time.

I ran four rounds of prototype testing with the canvas interaction model, iterating on the drag-and-drop UX until it felt genuinely fluid rather than forced.

The Preference Engine. Rather than a lengthy onboarding quiz, I designed a progressive preference system: the first time a user saved a place, they'd see a small set of contextual tags — "quiet", "good for solo", "worth a queue". Over time, the system used these implicit signals to surface better recommendations.

This approach meant new users got immediate value without needing to invest upfront, while returning users benefited from an experience that improved with use.

04 — Results

From guide to destination

3.2×Increase in average session depth post-launch
68%Of beta users created a second trip within 30 days
12kCurated place recommendations now accessible via the canvas

The platform launched to a waitlist of 4,200 users in autumn 2021. The co-founders described it as the first time the Meridian content library felt "alive" rather than static. The progressive preference system proved particularly sticky — users who interacted with three or more preference tags had a 2.8× higher 90-day retention rate than those who didn't.
