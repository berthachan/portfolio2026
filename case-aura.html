Aura E-Commerce App

Rethinking the mobile shopping experience for a curated lifestyle brand, resulting in a 40% lift in conversion within the first month of launch.

01 — The Challenge

A beautiful brand with a broken funnel

Aura had strong brand equity and a loyal customer base, but their mobile app — originally built by a dev shop in 2019 — had never been properly designed. It was functional but joyless, and analytics showed a 74% drop-off at the product detail page.

The founder, Marcus Chen, wanted an experience that matched the brand's premium positioning: tactile, confident, and editorially curated. Not another generic commerce template.

The core design challenge was the onboarding and discovery flow. New users couldn't understand Aura's unique curation model within the first few screens, and existing users found the search and filter system unusable on mobile.

My brief was to redesign the end-to-end mobile experience, from first launch through repeat purchase, with a specific focus on the product discovery and checkout funnel.

02 — Research

Understanding the gap between desire and action

I began with a two-week discovery sprint: analysing FullStory session recordings, running moderated usability tests with 8 existing customers, and conducting a heuristic audit of the existing app against Nielsen's principles and platform guidelines.

The session recordings were revelatory. Users would spend 3–4 minutes on a product page, scrolling repeatedly, then leave without adding to cart. Exit surveys pointed to ambiguity: users weren't sure if items were genuinely limited edition, didn't trust the sizing information, and couldn't quickly compare products.

I mapped these friction points to a revised user journey and defined three core design principles for the new experience: confidence, clarity, and delight.

03 — Design Decisions

Four critical changes that moved the needle

Product Detail Redesign. I rebuilt the PDP from scratch, prioritising trust signals: a persistent scarcity indicator, a revised size guide with fit notes, and a sticky CTA that didn't require scrolling back to the top. Image carousels were replaced with a swipeable full-bleed gallery.

Onboarding Flow. A new three-screen onboarding introduced the curation model, allowing users to select their aesthetic preferences. This data fed a personalised home feed and reduced first-session bounce by 31%.

Discovery Overhaul. The filter system was replaced with a visual faceted search: instead of dropdown menus, users tapped large visual chips organised by mood, occasion, and material. Filter application was instant with no page reload.

Micro-interactions. I designed a suite of small moments — an add-to-cart animation, a haptic-linked wishlist toggle, a personalised "You might also love" section powered by browsing behaviour. These increased session depth by 2.4× on average.

04 — Reflection

What I'd do differently

The biggest learning from this project was the value of quantitative data as a starting point for qualitative research. The session recordings told me where users were failing; the interviews told me why. Neither alone would have surfaced the sizing-trust issue that turned out to be the single highest-impact fix.

If I were starting again, I'd invest more time in the repeat-purchase experience. We focused heavily on acquisition and first-purchase conversion, but retention data from the six months post-launch showed that the re-engagement flow needed as much attention as the initial funnel. That work is ongoing.
